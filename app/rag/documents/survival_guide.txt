BASIC SURVIVAL GUIDE

CHAPTER 1: WATER PROCUREMENT AND PURIFICATION
Finding clean water is the most critical survival priority after immediate safety. The human body can survive roughly three days without water. Look for flowing streams, collect rainwater, or dig for groundwater in dry riverbeds. Always purify water before drinking by boiling it for at least one minute at sea level (three minutes above 6,500 feet). Chemical purification tablets containing iodine or chlorine dioxide are lightweight alternatives. Solar disinfection (SODIS) works by placing clear water in transparent bottles under direct sunlight for six hours.

CHAPTER 2: SHELTER CONSTRUCTION
Shelter protects against hypothermia, hyperthermia, rain, and wind. Prioritize building shelter before nightfall. A debris hut requires minimal tools: create an A-frame with a ridgepole supported by a tree or forked branch, then layer leaves, bark, and branches over the frame. In snow, dig a snow cave or build a quinzhee by piling snow, letting it sinter for two hours, then hollowing it out. Always insulate the ground beneath you with pine boughs, leaves, or a sleeping pad.

CHAPTER 3: FIRE STARTING
Fire provides warmth, light, water purification, cooking, signaling, and psychological comfort. Gather tinder (dry grass, bark shavings, dryer lint), kindling (small twigs), and fuel (larger branches and logs) before attempting ignition. Use a ferro rod, matches, or lighter. In wet conditions, find dry material inside standing dead trees or under dense canopy. A fire lay such as the teepee or log cabin structure promotes airflow for reliable ignition.

CHAPTER 4: FOOD PROCUREMENT
The rule of threes states a person can survive roughly three weeks without food. Prioritize calorie-dense and easily identifiable food sources. Insects like crickets, grasshoppers, and ants are safe and protein-rich. Learn to identify at least five edible wild plants in your region. Fishing with improvised hooks (thorns, safety pins) or traps is more calorie-efficient than active hunting. Never eat mushrooms unless you are 100% certain of identification. Cook all wild game and fish thoroughly.

CHAPTER 5: NAVIGATION WITHOUT GPS
Use the sun: it rises roughly in the east and sets roughly in the west. A stick shadow method creates a basic east-west line. At night in the Northern Hemisphere, locate Polaris (the North Star) by following the pointer stars of the Big Dipper. In the Southern Hemisphere, use the Southern Cross. Moss growth is unreliable for navigation. Follow waterways downstream to increase chances of finding civilization.

CHAPTER 6: FIRST AID BASICS
Stop severe bleeding by applying direct pressure with a clean cloth. If bleeding does not stop, apply a tourniquet two to three inches above the wound, note the time, and do not remove it. Clean wounds with clean water. Improvised splints can be made from sticks and cloth for suspected fractures — immobilize the joint above and below the break. For burns, cool with clean running water for at least 10 minutes. Do not pop blisters. Signs of shock include pale skin, rapid pulse, and confusion — lay the person flat and elevate their legs.

CHAPTER 7: SIGNALING FOR RESCUE
Three of anything is a universal distress signal: three fires, three whistle blasts, three rock piles. A signal mirror can be seen up to 50 miles away on a clear day. Use ground-to-air signals: a large "X" means requiring medical assistance, "V" means requiring assistance, and "→" indicates direction of travel. Bright clothing or markers improve visibility. Stay near your last known location when possible.

CHAPTER 8: PSYCHOLOGICAL SURVIVAL
The will to survive is the most important survival tool. Use the STOP method: Sit, Think, Observe, Plan. Maintain a routine to preserve mental health. Set small achievable goals each day. Stay busy with camp improvements, fire maintenance, or skill practice. Loneliness and fear are normal; acknowledge them without letting them drive decisions. Singing, talking aloud, or journaling can help maintain psychological stability.

CHAPTER 9: DISASTER-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE
Earthquake: Drop, Cover, Hold On. After shaking stops, move to open ground away from damaged structures. Watch for aftershocks.
Flood: Move to higher ground immediately. Never attempt to walk or drive through floodwaters. Six inches of moving water can knock you down.
Wildfire: Evacuate early. If trapped, find a body of water, ditch, or cleared area. Cover exposed skin and breathe through a wet cloth.
Hurricane/Tornado: Shelter in an interior room on the lowest floor. Stay away from windows.
Nuclear/Chemical: Move upwind and uphill. Remove contaminated clothing. Shelter in place with sealed windows if evacuation is not possible.

CHAPTER 10: URBAN SURVIVAL
In urban disaster scenarios, secure your immediate area. Locate water from water heaters, toilet tanks (not bowls), and canned goods. Avoid downed power lines — assume all are live. Mark cleared rooms with spray paint or tape. Travel in groups when possible. Barricade entry points for security. Keep a go-bag with water, food, first aid, flashlight, radio, and copies of important documents.
